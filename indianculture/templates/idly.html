Idli-Sambar

History:

The history of Idli-Sambar dates back centuries and is deeply rooted in South Indian culinary traditions. Idli, the steamed rice cake, is believed to have originated in present-day Karnataka. It is mentioned in ancient Indian texts, indicating its long-standing presence in the region. Over time, the dish spread to other parts of South India, becoming an integral part of the breakfast culture. Sambar, the lentil-based vegetable stew, also has ancient origins. It is associated with the Maratha ruler Shahuji, who is said to have introduced the dish to the Thanjavur Maratha kingdom in the 18th century. Since then, Sambar has become a ubiquitous accompaniment in South Indian cuisine.

Places Famous for Idli-Sambar:

1. Chennai, Tamil Nadu: Chennai, the capital of Tamil Nadu, is renowned for its vibrant breakfast culture, and Idli-Sambar is a staple in the city. Local eateries and restaurants in Chennai serve this classic combination with variations to suit different tastes. 2. Bangalore, Karnataka: In Bangalore, Idli-Sambar is a popular breakfast choice, reflecting the city's diverse culinary influences. The dish is readily available in eateries ranging from traditional breakfast joints to modern cafes. 3. Hyderabad, Telangana: While famous for its Biryani, Hyderabad also has a thriving breakfast scene, and Idli-Sambar is commonly enjoyed by locals and visitors alike. 4. Kochi, Kerala: Kerala, though known for its unique breakfast items like Appam and Puttu, has embraced Idli-Sambar as a popular and convenient morning meal.

Preparation: Idli:

1. Ingredients: The batter is made from a combination of fermented rice and urad dal (black gram). 2. Fermentation: The batter is left to ferment overnight, resulting in a light and fluffy texture. 3. Steaming: The batter is poured into idli molds and steamed until the idlis are cooked through, creating soft, spongy rice cakes. Sambar: 1. Ingredients: Sambar is made with pigeon peas (toor dal), tamarind pulp, a blend of spices, and a variety of vegetables like drumsticks, carrots, and eggplant. 2. Cooking: The dal and vegetables are cooked together with tamarind pulp and spices until they form a flavorful and aromatic stew.
